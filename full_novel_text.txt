 Chapter1 : Serena and the Fear of Being Seen At Hangzhou International Academy, the rain always made the campus look more serious than it really was. The glass walls of the main building blurred the willow trees outside, and the wet stone paths reflected the pale sky like a quiet mirror. Serena liked that kind of weather. It made everything seem softer, as if the world had agreed to speak in a lower voice. She sat by the window in Class 11A, her posture straight, her notes arranged in a neat stack, each color of pen placed with almost ritual precision. To most people, Serena looked calm. Maybe even cold. But inside, she was often anxious in a way she would never admit out loud. Not because she feared failure exactly, but because she feared being seen failing. That was different. Failure could be fixed. Embarrassment could be hidden. But being noticed while imperfect—that felt like standing under a spotlight without a script. “Serena,” her deskmate Lin Xiaoyan whispered, leaning over carefully, “did Mr. Chen say the quiz is today or tomorrow?” Xiaoyan was always a little apprehensive （ 担忧的） , her eyebrows slightly lifted, as if the world might suddenly ask her something she had not prepared for. She studied hard, more diligent than almost anyone Serena knew, but her grade stayed in the middle. Serena liked her because she did not pretend to be effortless. She was honest about being tired, honest about being confused, honest about needing help. That honesty felt authentic （ 真的） in a classroom where many students performed confidence like a school play. “Tomorrow,” Serena said. “You still have time.” Xiaoyan sighed in relief. “You always sound so sure.” Serena almost laughed. She was not sure. She simply looked sure, which was often enough. Across the room, the class monitor, Ethan Zhou, stood near the whiteboard discussing the upcoming TED-style speech event with a few boys. He had the kind of leadership that did not need permission. He was ambitious , intelligent, and effortlessly articulate （ 善于表达的） , as if every sentence had been polished before arriving in his mouth. In Class 11A, which had thirty students—sixteen girls and fourteen boys—everyone knew Ethan could organize group work, settle arguments, and still make it look easy. He liked being admired, and to be honest, Serena had admired him too. For a while, that admiration had become something more dangerous. It had lasted only one or two months, just enough time to feel real. They had exchanged late-night messages, small jokes in the corridor, a few lingering glances that said more than words. Ethan had seemed to belong to every room he entered. Serena, by contrast, often felt like she was standing just outside the glass. When he smiled at her, she had once thought that maybe she could finally stop restraining （ 抑制） herself so tightly. But then he ended it—gently, neatly, almost too neatly. He said he liked her, but he was busy, uncertain, not ready. His reasons had been soft enough to be polite and sharp enough to cut. Serena had not cried in front of him. She had only nodded and gone quiet. Later, alone in her room, she had felt the grief rise slowly, like cold water filling a sealed container. Now, weeks later, the ache had faded into something more brittle : not broken, exactly, but easily cracked. The worst part was that life kept moving. Girls in the class formed small circles with almost theatrical speed. There was the skincare group, the manga group, the weekend shopping group, the girls who spoke only in half-joking English slang, the girls who laughed too loudly at one another’s mistakes. Serena could never decide whether she wanted to join them or run from them. She wanted belonging , but she disliked the way those groups seemed to conform to invisible rules. Who sat where. Who talked to whom. Who was “in” and who was quietly left out. She found that behavior chaotic and strangely childish, though she would never say it aloud. It made her resent the social system of school, where popularity often felt like a hidden currency. At lunch, she sat with Xiaoyan and two other girls, while the rest of the class filled the cafeteria with noise. Serena listened more than she spoke. When she did speak, her words were often careful, discreet , and measured. She knew how to be candid when necessary, but she also knew that honesty could make people uncomfortable. In a room full of soft alliances, one blunt sentence could make you conspicuous . “Are you practicing your speech tonight?” Xiaoyan asked. Serena nodded. “A little.” “A little?” Xiaoyan smiled. “You mean eight times.” Serena did not deny it. She was a perfectionist , and she hated that word because it made her sound proud, when in reality it felt like a burden . If she did something, she wanted it done beautifully. Not because she loved excellence for its own sake, but because excellence made her harder to criticize. If she was flawless, no one could call her weak, ordinary, or forgettable. And if she was not perfect, she felt vulnerable . That afternoon, an announcement came over the intercom: the school would hold a TED-style speaking event the following week. Each class had nominated one student, but this year, because of the event’s reputation, the organizers had opened a second round of auditions. Students could still apply. Serena’s pen stopped movin g. The topic list appeared on the screen in the hallway later that day. She read them twice, then a third time. When she saw “Impostor Syndrome” and the parenthetical subtitle— the fear of being seen —something in her chest tightened. It was as if the school had named the private shape of her mind without asking permission. That night, in her bedroom overlooking a row of damp plane trees, Serena sat at her desk and stared at a blank page. The room was quiet , but her thoughts were not. She wanted to speak. She wanted to prove herself. She wanted to be recognized for something real, something that did not depend on charm or social performance. Her grades were above average, sometimes even excellent, but she could never settle on a clear future goal. Maybe she studied partly because she cared, and partly because she wanted approval from the world that always seemed slightly out of reach. She wrote one sentence. Then another. The more she wrote, the more she realized that the fear she had spent years hiding was not a weakness. It was a wound, yes, but also a source of truth. She had been living as if visibility were a threat, when maybe it could become a choice. A notification lit up her phone. It was from Ethan. Are you applying for TED? Serena stared at the screen, her pulse suddenly too loud in the silence. Below the message, before she could answer, another notification appeared—from an unknown number. Hi. I’m from C Class. I heard you might speak on impostor syndrome. I’m speaking too. My topic is “Who am I?” Serena’s fingers froze. She did not know it yet, but the second message was about to change the entire shape of her spring. Chapter2 : The Unknown Message Serena saw the message in the middle of second period, when the classroom was in one of its strangely quiet moods. Mr. Chen was writing formulas on the board, Xiaoyan was underlining something with nervous concentration, and the sound of rain against the windows had become almost hypnotic. The phone in Serena’s pocket vibrated once, then again, and her heart answered before her mind did. She waited until Mr. Chen turned to face the board, then glanced down. Hi. I’m from C Class. I heard you might speak on impostor syndrome. I’m speaking too. My topic is “Who am I?” There was no name under the message. No emoji. No unnecessary punctuation. Just ten plain words that somehow felt more compelling than any polished introduction. Serena read them twice. Then she read them again. A part of her was immediately reluctant to respond. Unknown boys were always a little dangerous, not because they were dangerous in the obvious sense, but because they could create disorder in a life that Serena preferred to keep precise. She did not like surprises. Surprises were emotionally awkward, socially unstable, and impossible to rehearse. Yet the message was not pushy. It was almost discreet , as if the sender understood the value of not making himself too visible. That, strangely, made him more noticeable. Xiaoyan leaned over. “What are you looking at?” “Nothing,” Serena said too quickly. Xiaoyan’s expression changed. Not judgment exactly. Just alertness. Xiaoyan was observant in the way shy people often are: she noticed what others pretended not to notice. Serena locked her screen. But the message had already entered her mind and settled there, conspicuous in its simplicity. During lunch, she found herself thinking about it while stirring her noodles. The words “Who am I?” seemed simple, even childish, and yet the question felt strangely vast. Serena had spent so long trying to become a version of herself that others would approve of that she had never fully examined the difference between approval and identity. Was she studying hard because she truly wanted knowledge, or because she wanted recognition? Was her desire to speak on stage an authentic calling, or merely an endeavor to avoid being ordinary? It sounded like a person who had lived with many borders. Serena read it slowly. She had the sudden, irrational sense that the sender was standing somewhere nearby, watching the way she held the phone, waiting to see whether she would answer. The thought was absurd. Also deeply disconcerting. She typed: Sure. When? The reply came almost instantly. Tomorrow after school, at the café by the gate. I’ll be the one with the blue notebook. She stared at the screen. Blue notebook. That detail felt almost minuscule, and yet it mattered. It meant he had planned ahead. He had noticed what he wanted to be noticed. He had chosen an object small enough to seem natural, but specific enough to create memory. Serena should have ignored it. A pragmatic person might have simply agreed out of convenience, then treated the meeting as a harmless academic exchange. But Serena could already feel the scene taking shape in her mind: the rain, the café window, the notebook, the voice she had not heard yet. And beneath that image was another, deeper question she did not want to name. What if this conversation became the start of something she could not control? Outside, the rain intensified. The campus trees blurred into dark green shapes, and the sky looked suddenly heavier, as if it were holding too much weather at once. Serena watched the water stream down the windowpane and thought of the word saturation. The air, the light, the silence—all of it felt saturated with something unspoken. Later that evening, at home, her mother asked from the kitchen, “You’re quiet today. TED application?” Serena answered after a pause. “I got selected.” Her mother’s voice softened. “That’s good.” But her mother did not look surprised. Serena’s mother had always understood her daughter’s innate seriousness, the way she moved through life as if every choice carried invisible consequences. She also understood the pressure Serena put on herself, though perhaps not fully. To her mother, this was all part of Serena’s parental-shaped world too: grades, reputation, future, safety. “Will you be practicing with classmates?” her mother asked. “Maybe.” “Try to take it seriously,” her mother said, though the word seriously in her mother’s mouth meant something closer to discipline than to fear. Serena nodded and returned to her room. On her desk lay the first draft of her speech, only a page long. The title at the top was still too bold, too exposed: The Fear of Being Seen She touched the paper lightly, as if it were made of something fragile. Then she opened a new document and began to write again. This time, however, she was no longer only writing for the judges, or the school, or even for herself. Somewhere across campus, in another classroom with another window and another rain-slicked view of Hangzhou, a boy named Leo was preparing to speak about being foreign everywhere. And for the first time in a long while, Serena felt the shape of her own life shift just a little—like a door opening by a few inches, enough to let in air, not yet enough to escape. The next afternoon, she knew, she would walk to the café by the gate. And she had no idea what, exactly, she was walking toward. Chapter3 : A Voice from C Class The café by the school gate was smaller than Serena had imagined, with one narrow shelf of pastries and windows that fogged at the edges from the damp air outside. She arrived five minutes early, which was, in her mind, the only acceptable way to arrive anywhere that mattered. Her umbrella dripped quietly by the door while she chose a seat with a clear view of both the entrance and the counter. She was already scrutinizing the room before she realized she was doing it. There were three other students inside, all from different classes, each absorbed in a laptop or a phone. The place smelled faintly of coffee, warm bread, and wet coats. It was ordinary, but Serena felt unusually aware of every small detail: the sugar packets stacked unevenly, the sound of a spoon tapping porcelain, the pale reflection of her own face in the window. At 4:12, a boy in a dark coat stepped inside carrying a blue notebook. He was not especially tall. Not especially flashy. In fact, his first impression was almost unassuming. But the moment Serena saw him, she understood why Leo’s message had felt so different from everyone else’s. He moved without hurry, as if he had no need to prove that he belonged in the room. That calmness was strangely captivating. He looked up, found her immediately, and gave a small nod. “Serena?” His voice was low, clear, and unexpectedly melodic. It did not force itself into the space. It simply entered it, like rain through an open window. “Yes.” Serena stood, then regretted the stiffness of the movement. “You’re Leo.” “Guilty.” The joke was light, almost dry. Not too much. Not too little. Serena found herself compelled to smile. He took the seat across from her and placed the blue notebook on the table with a gesture so careful it almost looked deliberate. “Thanks for coming. I know TED prep is probably chaos right now.” “Probably,” Serena said. Leo studied her for a second, but not in a rude way. More like someone trying to confer meaning from what he saw. Serena did not like being looked at for too long, but his attention felt discreet enough not to threaten her. “I heard your topic title,” he said. “The fear of being seen. That’s a strong line.” Serena lowered her gaze to the table. “It’s only a working title.” “Still strong.” She should have answered with something neutral. Instead, she heard herself say, “Yours is stronger.” Leo tilted his head. “Who am I?” “Yes. It’s… direct.” “And maybe a little vague,” he said with a half-smile. That was true. Serena almost laughed at the accuracy of it. The topic was simple in the most counterintuitive way: the more open it was, the harder it seemed to answer. A question so basic could still be deeply disorienting. Leo opened the notebook. Inside were pages of handwriting, arrows, underlines, and tiny notes in the margins. The structure was intricate, but not messy. It had the order of someone who had thought about his words for a long time. “I’m trying to talk about place,” he said. “Not just geography. More like… emotional geography.” Serena looked up. He continued, “I grew up in Belgium. People there saw me as Chinese. When I came back here, people saw me as foreign. It made me realize I had never really had a place where my identity felt settled.” He said it plainly, without dramatics. That plainness made it more powerful. Serena felt a sudden pressure behind her ribs, as if something in his words had touched a hidden bruise. She knew what it was like to feel split between versions of yourself. Not by country, but by expectation. At school, she was the student who performed well. At home, the daughter who kept things under control. Online, she became more conspicuous than she ever was in person, because texts allowed her to shape her image. But none of those versions felt complete. Leo’s story gave that feeling a name. A waiter set down two cups of tea. The steam rose between them, soft and temporary. “You’re very perceptive,” Serena said after a pause. Leo gave a shrug. “Maybe just tired of being misunderstood.” That answer was so simple it almost seemed like a reveal. It made him feel more human, less like the polished boy who could show up with a blue notebook and know exactly how to enter a conversation. And yet he remained confident in the quietest possible way, not the kind of confidence that demanded applause, but the kind that existed because he had already survived enough uncertainty to stop apologizing for it. Serena had spent years believing confidence had to look loud. Ethan’s confidence had been loud. Class monitors, debate captains, student council leaders—all of them often looked as if they had been granted a public script. Leo’s confidence was different. More reflective. More private. More real, perhaps. She hated how quickly she was noticing the difference. “What about you?” Leo asked. “How do you write about impostor syndrome without sounding like a textbook?” Serena looked down at the tea. “I’m not sure yet.” “Good. That’s honest.” She thought of how many times she had tried to present herself as complete when she was still becoming. The truth was more authentic than perfection, but truth also made her feel vulnerable. If she admitted uncertainty, then she could no longer hide behind competence. There would be no clean performance. And yet there was something soothing in Leo’s way of speaking. He did not demand that she simplify herself. He seemed to understand that identity was not a single answer but a series of choices, tensions, and revisions. The idea was oddly sustaining. They talked for nearly an hour. He described Belgium in fragments: brick streets, winter light, a teacher who mispronounced his name for three years, classmates who asked where he was “really” from. Serena described her own school life in safer terms: class dynamics, TED auditions, her frustration with group work, the way girls formed little islands of friendship and then acted as if the whole sea belonged to them. He had the rare ability to make conversation feel like conveying thought instead of defending it. She found that strangely relieving. Then he said, “I think the hardest part is when people want you to proclaim who you are before you’re ready.” Serena went still. Because that was exactly it. She had always wanted recognition, but she also feared the moment of declaration. If she said who she was too early, it might turn into a performance. If she waited too long, someone else would define her for her. The sentence lodged inside her mind like a key in a lock. Outside, rain slid down the glass in thin silver lines. A bus passed the gate, its headlights briefly washing the café in pale light. For a second Serena could see her own reflection clearly. She looked serious, almost severe. But beneath that, there was a girl who wanted very much to be understood without being reduced. Leo closed his notebook. “I’m going to the library after this. Want to walk back together?” The question was ordinary. Too ordinary, perhaps. But Serena felt its weight anyway. Not because it was dangerous, but because it was simple enough to change the shape of an afternoon. She should have said no. She knew she should have stayed discreet, kept distance, controlled the emotional variables. Yet she heard herself answer, “Sure.” They left the café and walked slowly back toward campus. The rain had eased into a mist, and the path glistened under the streetlights. Serena kept one hand around the strap of her bag, the other inside her coat pocket, aware of every step. At the corner by the old plane tree, Leo glanced at her and said, “Your speech topic might be about being seen, but I think your real struggle is being recognized for who you actually are.” Serena stopped walking. The sentence was so accurate it felt almost jarring. He did not move away. He simply looked at her with the same calm expression. And for one suspended moment, Serena felt as if he had seen straight through the version of herself she offered the world. That was not comforting. It was terrifying. But it was also, somehow, the beginning of something.
